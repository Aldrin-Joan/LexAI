LEGAL OPINION

TO: Whomsoever It May Concern
FROM: Senior Advocate, Supreme Court of India
SUBJECT: Summary of the Right to Privacy in India
DATE: March 29, 2026

I. INTRODUCTION
The right to privacy is now a firmly established fundamental right under the Constitution of India. This opinion provides a concise summary based on the landmark precedents of the Supreme Court.

II. THE FUNDAMENTAL RIGHT TO PRIVACY
In the case of Justice K.S.Puttaswamy(Retd) And Anr. vs Union Of India And Ors. (2017), a nine-judge bench of the Supreme Court unanimously declared that the right to privacy is an intrinsic part of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 and as a part of the freedoms guaranteed by Part III of the Constitution.

III. EVOLUTION AND EARLIER PRECEDENTS
Prior to the Puttaswamy judgment, the court had addressed privacy in several instances:
1. Govind vs State Of Madhya Pradesh (1975): The Court recognized a limited right to privacy under Article 21, specifically regarding police surveillance, but noted it was not absolute.
2. P.R. Metrani vs Commissioner Of Income Tax (1996): The Court discussed the implications of search and seizure on an individual's privacy and the need for procedural safeguards.

IV. SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
The Supreme Court has held that the right to privacy includes:
1. Individual autonomy over personal choices.
2. Protection of personal information.
3. Freedom from unauthorized governmental intrusion.

However, the right is not absolute and can be restricted if the state meets a three-fold test:
1. Legality (existence of a law).
2. Need (legitimate state aim).
3. Proportionality (rational nexus between the means and the goal).

V. CONCLUSION
The Right to Privacy is a foundational pillar of Indian democracy, ensuring that individuals maintain dignity and control over their personal lives against arbitrary state interference.
